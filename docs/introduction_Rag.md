# Introduction

Catastrophic health events (CHEs), whether caused by natural forces such as floods, hurricanes, or earthquakes, or by human actions such as radiological incidents, pose significant threats to human life, infrastructure, and economic stability. For example, the 2023 Turkey-Syria earthquakes resulted in over 56,000 deaths and 100,000 injuries, while the 2011 earthquake in Japan caused more than 15,000 fatalities, over 6,000 injuries, 2,585 missing persons, and an estimated \$235 billion in economic damage \citep{Amani_2025}. In the aftermath of such disasters, a large number of injured individuals require immediate medical attention. However, damaged healthcare infrastructure, including hospitals that may be non-functional or in need of evacuation, places extreme strain on response systems. This makes the efficient use of limited medical resources within the first few hours following a disaster absolutely critical. Casualty response planning (CRP) plays a key role in this process, coordinating multiple agencies to allocate resources, treat the injured, and transport patients to operational medical facilities.

Alternative care facilities (ACFs) are widely used to manage the surge in casualties following disasters. The primary goal of ACFs is to expand healthcare capacity when hospitals are overwhelmed and unable to accommodate the large number of injured individuals. However, their effectiveness depends heavily on strategic decisions about where they are established and how resources are allocated. For instance, after the 1995 Sarin attack in Tokyo’s subway system, the lack of a coordinated patient distribution plan, along with poor communication and the geographical dispersion of victims, led to significant delays. Some ambulances spent precious time searching for available beds, while others transported patients to nearby hospitals without verifying capacity or mistakenly chose distant facilities \citep{Larson_2006}. As highlighted by \cite{Larson_2006}, cities must develop clear procedures for assigning patients to hospitals based on their conditions, locations, and real-time hospital capacity.

Compounding this challenge, hospitals are not always spared in disaster scenarios—they can themselves become victims. Emergency plans often assume hospitals remain operational, but this is not always the case. In fact, many healthcare facilities are located in hazard-prone regions. For example, it is estimated that between 50\% and 70\% of healthcare facilities in Latin America and the Caribbean are situated in high-risk zones, with similar figures reported in the United Kingdom \citep{Bagaria_2009}. When hospitals are compromised, casualty response becomes far more complex, and the need for robust evacuation plans becomes critical. Despite the importance of hospital evacuation planning, there is still no universally accepted approach, and existing guidelines vary significantly. For example, the Center for Bioterrorism Preparedness and Planning recommends evacuating the most critical patients first, whereas the Association for periOperative Registered Nurses and the New York Centers for Terrorism Preparedness and Planning suggest starting with the least critical \citep{Childers_2010}. Real-world cases reveal the consequences of inadequate planning. In 1997, after a chemical explosion forced Helena Regional Medical Center to evacuate, high-priority patients were successfully transferred to other hospitals, but with no clear plan for low-priority patients, they were relocated to a local community college, where cafeteria tables were used as makeshift hospital beds \citep{Squillace_2010}. These challenges underscore the urgent need for integrated planning that addresses both ACF establishment and hospital evacuation in the disaster response phase.
